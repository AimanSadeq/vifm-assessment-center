## 0. Ipsative vs. normative measurement — the choice, and my recommendation

This is the response-format decision, and it has direct consequences for the
norm-group question raised earlier.

| | **Normative** (rating scale) | **Ipsative** (forced-choice) |
|---|---|---|
| **How it works** | Rate each statement on its own scale (e.g., 1–5 Likert) | Choose/rank among options balanced for desirability ("most / least like me") |
| **Score meaning** | Absolute level, **comparable across people** | **Relative strength within the person** (scores sum to a constant) |
| **Faking / social desirability** | Vulnerable (also acquiescence, halo) | Strongly resistant |
| **Norm-referencing (percentiles vs peers)** | Supported directly | **Broken** for classic ipsative — can't be normed properly |
| **Reliability / correlations** | Standard methods apply | Distorted by the ipsative constraint |

**The modern resolution — Thurstonian IRT (TIRT) forced-choice.** TIRT recovers
**quasi-normative, between-person-comparable** scores *from* forced-choice items —
keeping the faking resistance **and** restoring the ability to norm. This is
current best practice for high-stakes forced-choice, and it's exactly what
Caliber's `psychometrics-proposal.md` already points to.

**Recommendation (by use case):**
1. **Selection / pre-hire (high-stakes):** **forced-choice scored with
   Thurstonian IRT.** Faking resistance *and* normatively comparable, rankable
   scores.
2. **Development / 360 / Reflect:** **normative Likert** — simpler, easier to give
   feedback on.
3. **If clients want peer percentiles (the norm-group ask):** use **normative** or
   **TIRT-recovered** scores — **avoid classic ipsative**, which cannot be normed.
   (Either way, the percentiles still need an actual norm sample first.)
4. **Knowledge / SJT items** are scored objectively against a key
   (criterion-referenced) — a separate axis. Note the **MOST/LEAST SJT format
   below is structurally forced-choice**, so it inherits ipsative-style faking
   resistance at the item level.

Below, each competency has **(A) a criterion-keyed SJT item** (MOST/LEAST
forced-choice) and **(B) a behavioural interview (CBI) question** scored against
BARS. The ipsative-vs-normative choice bites hardest on the **self-report
scales** (see `competency-self-report-cluster1.md`), authored in both forms.

> **Test-design note:** SJT options are shown A–D with the key stated; **at
> delivery, option order is randomised**. SJT keys are theory/expert-seeded —
> confirm with an SME effectiveness panel + pilot item stats before high-stakes
> use.

## 1. Forward Strategy Setting
*Research basis: Liedtka (1998); Mintzberg (1994).*

**(A) SJT.** You lead a team. A competitor's new approach is winning over younger
customers, and an industry shift is expected within ~18 months. Leadership wants
this quarter's targets protected. MOST / LEAST effective:
- A. Keep the team focused on this quarter's targets; revisit the threat next year.
- B. Run a scenario analysis of the shift and shape a 2–3 year response **while**
  protecting near-term targets.
- C. Immediately divert investment into an untested new offering.
- D. Wait for competitors to move, then copy the winning approach.

**Key:** MOST = **B**; LEAST = **A**.
**(B) CBI.** "Tell me about a time you set a direction for your team that looked
beyond immediate targets to account for a change in the market or rules."
*Probes:* What signals did you read? How far ahead did you plan? What did you trade
off?

## 2. Commercial & Market Awareness
*Research basis: Wagner & Sternberg (1985).*

**(A) SJT.** A long-standing client/partner asks you to expand a commitment.
Conditions in their main market have worsened sharply. MOST / LEAST:
- A. Agree based on the long relationship and good history.
- B. Assess how the changed conditions affect their position before deciding.
- C. Refuse outright to avoid any risk.
- D. Agree to a smaller, conditional commitment tied to confirmed milestones.

**Key:** MOST = **B**; LEAST = **A** (D partial credit).
**(B) CBI.** "Describe a decision where reading the wider market or economic
context changed what you recommended." *Probes:* What signals mattered? What would
the naïve call have been?

## 3. Financial Literacy & Acumen
*Research basis: Lusardi & Mitchell (2014).*

**(A) Objective item — single best answer.** A business unit reports rising
revenue, but operating costs are rising faster, its cash buffer has dropped
sharply, and overdue receivables have doubled. Most accurate interpretation?
- A. Revenue growth alone shows the unit is healthy.
- B. Despite higher revenue, deteriorating margins, cash, and receivables signal
  rising financial risk. ✅
- C. The cash drop doesn't matter while revenue grows.
- D. Rising receivables are simply a positive sign of more sales.

**Key:** **B** (objective 1/0). *The purest "objective" item — a single
veridically correct reading.*
**(B) CBI.** "Walk me through a time you used financial data to challenge or
support a business decision." *Probes:* Which indicators? What trade-off did the
numbers reveal?

## 4. Critical Analysis
*Research basis: Facione (1990); Halpern (1998).*

**(A) SJT.** A report states: "Team X is our best performer — it has the highest
growth." Team X serves a new, fast-growing segment. MOST / LEAST:
- A. Accept it and roll out Team X's tactics everywhere.
- B. Check whether the growth comes from the segment/market vs. the team's
  practices before drawing lessons.
- C. Dismiss the report — new teams always grow fast.
- D. Rank all teams by growth and reward the top quartile.

**Key:** MOST = **B**; LEAST = **A**.
**(B) CBI.** "Tell me about a time you questioned a conclusion that others had
accepted. What did you find?" *Probes:* What assumption did you test? What evidence
changed your view?

## 5. Sound Judgement
*Research basis: Kahneman & Klein (2009).*

**(A) SJT.** A possible serious problem appears in an important account or process.
Confirming it will take ~48 hours; acting now may harm a key relationship; doing
nothing risks a major breach. MOST / LEAST:
- A. Wait for full confirmation to avoid friction.
- B. Apply targeted, proportionate interim measures while expediting the check and
  informing the right people.
- C. Take the most drastic action immediately.
- D. Flag it but take no interim action.

**Key:** MOST = **B**; LEAST = **A**.
**(B) CBI.** "Describe a high-stakes decision you made with incomplete information
and time pressure." *Probes:* What did you weigh? What were the second-order
consequences? Would you decide the same again?

## Scoring & assembly notes

- **SJT keys** are theory/expert-seeded. Before high-stakes use, confirm each key
  with an SME effectiveness-rating panel and check item statistics on pilot data
  (difficulty, discrimination). No norm group needed for this.
- **CBI/BARS** items are criterion-referenced (rate against the role's target
  proficiency) — no norm group needed either.
- **Normative percentiles** can be generated later by accumulating scores into
  role-segmented norms — a report layer, not a prerequisite.
- **Sector packs:** swap the SJTs for a sector variant (e.g. finance & banking)
  without changing CBI, self-report, or scoring.
